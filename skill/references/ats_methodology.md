# The ATS-passing 1-page biotech CV — the "why"

Most biotech applications are read **twice**: first by an ATS (Applicant Tracking System — Workday, Greenhouse, Comeet, SuccessFactors…), then, if you pass, by a hiring scientist for ~15 seconds. You have to win both.

## What the ATS is doing
It parses your PDF into fields (name, roles, dates, skills) and scores keyword overlap with the JD. It **fails** on:
- multi-column layouts, tables, text boxes → text extracted out of order or dropped
- images/icons/logos → invisible to it
- content in headers/footers → often ignored
- creative section names → it can't find "Experience" if you call it "My Journey"

So: **single column, standard headings, real text, keywords verbatim.**

## What the human is doing (the 15 seconds)
They skim the **top third** — headline, summary, first role. They're asking one question: *"Does this person fit the thing I need done?"* So:
- the **headline mirrors the title they posted** (instant "yes, relevant")
- the **summary** answers the fit question in 3 lines
- the **first bullets of the most recent/relevant role** prove it

## The tailoring principle: one truth, re-prioritized
You don't rewrite reality per job — you **re-order and re-frame the same facts** so the relevant ones are on top. The same PhD + industry experience can read as a *bench scientist* CV or a *business-development* CV depending on:
- which headline you lead with,
- which bullets sit in the top third,
- which skill categories you list first,
- how you sub-title each role to bridge toward the target.

(See the two `examples/` — identical facts, two framings.)

## The 4-check pre-send pass (works with no tools)
1. **Mirror the title** — exact posted title in your headline.
2. **Top-third rule** — the 3 bullets that matter most to *this* role are the first things read.
3. **Keyword match** — paste the JD into a word-frequency counter; make sure its top ~10 terms appear truthfully in yours.
4. **One-page discipline** — cut anything that doesn't argue for *this* role.

## Why one page (for most roles)
For non-academic biotech roles, a tight 1-pager signals judgment and respect for the reader's time. Keep a longer academic CV (with full publications) separately for faculty/postdoc applications; use the 1-pager for industry.
